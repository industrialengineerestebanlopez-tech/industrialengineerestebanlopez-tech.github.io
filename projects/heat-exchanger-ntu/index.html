PROBLEM Specification

Cool a hot process water stream (90 °C, 2.0 kg/s) using cooling water (20 °C, 2.5 kg/s) in a counter-flow exchanger with U = 1200 W/m²·K and 8 m² of area. Find the heat duty, outlet temperatures and effectiveness — and how the area would have to grow for a colder outlet.

METHOD ε-NTU, cross-checked with LMTD

The effectiveness of a counter-flow exchanger depends only on NTU and the capacity ratio Cr:

ε = (1 − e−NTU(1−Cr)) / (1 − Cr·e−NTU(1−Cr))   ,   Q = ε · Cmin · (Th,in − Tc,in) NTU = UA / Cmin  ·  Cr = Cmin / Cmax  ·  cross-check: Q = U·A·LMTD
Why this matters: ε-NTU rates an exchanger when outlet temperatures are unknown (no iteration), and the LMTD agreement (<1%) is an independent proof the calculation is right — the kind of check a reviewer looks for.

DESIGN Area–performance trade-off

Inverting the problem (required area for a target outlet) reveals diminishing returns: because effectiveness flattens at high NTU, cooling the hot stream below ~50 °C costs disproportionately more area. This is the curve that drives the sizing decision.

Engineering reading: the current 8 m² already sits on the “knee” of the ε-NTU curve. Pushing the hot outlet from 51 °C down to 45 °C would require roughly double the area — usually not worth it unless the process demands it. Fouling (a lower U) shifts the operating point left, eroding effectiveness over time.

CONCLUSION What this enables

  • Size for a target duty. Pick area / U to hit the required heat duty without over-building.
  • Choose the configuration. Counter-flow reaches higher effectiveness than parallel-flow for the same area.
  • Plan for fouling. Quantify how a declining U erodes effectiveness, and set a cleaning interval.
  • Trade area vs pumping. The natural next step: add pressure-drop and pumping-power to optimise total cost.
