The efficiency gap: the ideal cycle would reach 50.8 %, but real compressor and turbine losses pull it down to 36.2 % — a far bigger drop than in a steam cycle, because of the back-work ratio (below).

OPTIMISE Optimum pressure ratio & back-work

Efficiency rises with pressure ratio, but net work peaks — there is an optimum rp for a given turbine-inlet temperature. And the compressor is expensive: it eats most of the turbine's output.

Back-work ratio = 56 % — the compressor consumes more than half of the turbine work, versus < 1 % for the pump in a steam (Rankine) cycle. That is why a few points of compressor/turbine efficiency swing the whole engine, and why turbine-inlet temperature (limited by blade materials and cooling) is the dominant design lever.

AEROSPACE LINK From gas turbine to jet engine

A turbojet is an open Brayton cycle

Replace the closed loop with the atmosphere and the same four processes appear: the inlet and compressor raise the air pressure (1→2), the combustor adds heat (2→3), and the turbine expands the gas (3→4) — but here the turbine only has to drive the compressor. All the remaining energy is left in the fast exhaust jet, which produces thrust:

F = ṁ · (Vexit − Vinlet) + (pe − p0)·Ae

The very same analysis sizes aircraft engines, auxiliary power units (APUs), and the gas turbines behind ground and marine power. CONCLUSION What this enables

  • Pick the pressure ratio. Net work peaks at an optimum rp for the chosen turbine-inlet temperature.
  • Chase TIT. Turbine-inlet temperature is the dominant lever — hence materials and blade cooling.
  • Understand the trade with Brayton vs Rankine. The 56 % back-work ratio explains the two cycles' very different behaviour.
  • Extend to real engines. Regeneration, intercooling, reheat — and combined Brayton–Rankine plants, the most efficient on Earth.
