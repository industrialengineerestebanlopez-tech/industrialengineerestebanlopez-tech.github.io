DEFINE The problem

Bosch records thousands of measurements as each part flows through its assembly line. The challenge: predict which parts will fail quality control so they can be caught early — on a dataset so imbalanced and so large that naive methods fail on both statistics and memory.

The feature names L{line}_S{station}_F{feature} are a value-stream map in disguise. A Pareto of measurement density shows where the line is most complex; the donut shows the modelling challenge.

Finding: lines L1 (513 sensors) and L3 (245) carry 78% of all measurements — the most complex, most-monitored stages. With only 0.58% defects, the model must be judged on MCC, not accuracy.

IMPROVE Rare-event defect model

Engineering judgement: at 0.58% defects, a model that predicts “everything passes” already scores 99.4% accuracy. The competition metric — and the correct one for rare-event quality — is the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC), which only rewards catching real defects.

CONTROL From model to line control

  • Focus SPC where the process is complex. Put the highest-defect-rate stations on lines L1 and L3 under control charts first.
  • Catch escapes, accept some false alarms. Tune the threshold to the MCC optimum or to the cost of a missed defect vs. an extra check.
  • Trace every failing part's route. Recurring station sequences among failures point to the stage to fix.
  • Poka-yoke at critical stations. Turn the model's top signals into in-line gates that stop defects, not just flag them.
  • PDCA at scale. Re-score MCC and the station defect map per shift; retrain as the line drifts.
