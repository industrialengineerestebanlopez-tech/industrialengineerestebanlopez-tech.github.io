Signature result: the back-work ratio is 0.88 % — the pump consumes almost nothing. That is the core advantage of a vapour power cycle over a gas-turbine (Brayton) cycle, where the compressor can eat 40–60 % of the turbine work.

OPTIMISE Three levers for efficiency

Sweeping each design variable (everything else fixed) quantifies the three classic ways to raise Rankine efficiency: higher superheat, higher boiler pressure, and lower condenser pressure.

The real constraint: raising boiler pressure and lowering condenser pressure both push the turbine exit wetter. Below ~88 % quality, water droplets erode the last turbine blades — which is exactly why real plants add reheat: it lifts efficiency and keeps the steam dry.

CONCLUSION What this enables

  • Fix the operating point. The analysis sets boiler pressure, superheat and condenser pressure for a target efficiency.
  • Know the physical limit. The wetness constraint at the turbine exit caps how far pressure/vacuum can be pushed.
  • Justify reheat & regeneration. The next design steps follow directly from the trade-off shown here.
  • Compare against Brayton. The <1 % back-work ratio quantifies why steam cycles dominate base-load power.
